Oregon Health & Science University

In August 2017, researchers at the Oregon Health & Science University successful edited the genomes of 12 fetuses who had the genomic sequence responsible for hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. HCM causes the heart to develop an abnormally thick heart muscle that could result in instant cardiac death and displays no symptoms. There is no cure, and affects 1 in 500 people worldwide.

The scientists designed the CRISPR/Cas9 complex to target the specific sequence of DNA that causes HCM. The CRISPR enzyme was injected into the cells, and the sequence was removed and replaced with the healthy sequence in 72% of the fetuses. The experiment is yet another study done that confirms that CRISPR can be used to successfully edit the genomes of humans. The fetuses were never planted in mothers to form a legitimate baby but shows the possibilities available for future experiments aimed at removing and replacing harmful genes from human embryos.

Harvard University-"Mammophant"

Researcher George Church from Harvard University has successful inserted 45 wooly mammoth genes into the skin cells of elephants. The researches utilized CRISPR/Cas9 and other biotechnology to create the genes and insert them into the elephant cells. Genes associated with small ear, subcutaneous fat, hair, blood, and many others were successfully incorporated into the genomes of elephant skin cells. They will not be creating an entire wooly mammoth, but they say they want to create these mammoth-elephant hybrids within the next ten years once artifical wombs are able to take care of a fetus until it is ready to be born.

This type of research shows the possibility of one day being able to "de-extinct" species that once lived on Earth. There are many ethical consequences of such an experiment but researchers are justifying it by saying they are attempting to help elephants from colder climates better adjust to their environment.
